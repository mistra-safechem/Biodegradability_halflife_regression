### HSBD Comments
* "HSDB (NLM) serves as a curated compilation of experimental environmental fate data but does not impose a standardized persistence classification. Among the literature sources considered, only the approach by Gramatica et al. introduces a semi‑quantitative framework for persistence ranking conceptually related to VEGA’s discretized class system, whereas Fenner et al., Prosser et al., and Khan et al. treat degradation half‑lives as continuous or probabilistic variables without defining fixed persistence classes."

* Although stored as floating‑point numbers, the air half‑life values in the dataset form a small, finite set of repeated constants (e.g. 0.2083, 0.7083, 2.2917, 7.0833, 22.9167, 70.8333, 229.17, 708.33, 2291.67 days).
    Key implication:
    These numbers function as category representatives, not as independently measured compound‑specific endpoints.

* The numerical precision is misleading: the data is effectively ordinal / binned, not continuous.

* Limitations of persistence endpoint resolution.
    Although atmospheric degradation half‑lives are reported as continuous numerical values, inspection of the compiled dataset reveals pronounced discretisation, with a limited set of repeated constants assigned to large numbers of chemically diverse substances. This pattern indicates that the endpoint values represent class‑level or category‑based persistence estimates rather than compound‑specific kinetic measurements. Consequently, the apparent numerical precision should not be interpreted as reflecting experimental resolution, and small differences between values within the same discrete level are not chemically meaningful. The use of such quantised endpoints may mask real intra‑class variability in atmospheric degradation behaviour and limits the ability to draw mechanistic conclusions or to evaluate subtle structure–degradation relationships. These characteristics should be taken into account when interpreting model performance and uncertainty, particularly for regression‑based analyses.

### Vega data comments
Datasets seem to originate from curated training and test sets developed for the VEGA quantitative persistence QSAR models for water, soil, and sediment compartments (IRFMN). These datasets comprise mono‑constituent organic substances annotated experimental half‑life values (log₁₀ days) together with model‑predicted values. Experimental values are derived primarily from ECHA registration dossiers and from the semi‑decade half‑life class system reported by Gouin et al. (2004), with extensive data and structure cleaning as described in the QMRFs.

* VEGA QMRF: Persistence (water) quantitative model (IRFMN) – v1.0.1 (2022) [\[QMRF_PERSISTENCE_WATER_REG_VEGA | PDF\]](https://www.vegahub.eu/vegahub-dwn/qmrf/QMRF_PERSISTENCE_WATER_REG.pdf)
* VEGA QMRF: Persistence (soil) quantitative model (IRFMN) – v1.0.1 (2022) [\[QMRF_PERSISTENCE_SOIL_REG_VEGA | PDF\]](https://www.vegahub.eu/vegahub-dwn/qmrf/QMRF_PERSISTENCE_SOIL_REG.pdf)
* VEGA QMRF: Persistence (sediment) quantitative model (IRFMN) – v1.0.1 (2022) [\[QMRF_PERSISTENCE_SEDIMENT_REG_VEGA | PDF\]](https://www.vegahub.eu/vegahub-dwn/qmrf/QMRF_PERSISTENCE_SEDIMENT_REG.pdf)
* Gouin T., Cousins I., Mackay D. (2004) Chemosphere, 56, 531–535. DOI: 10.1016/j.chemosphere.2004.04.018
